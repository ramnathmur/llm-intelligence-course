BPE Tokenisation From Scratch

How byte-pair encoding builds the vocabulary that feeds every modern LLM

~15 min
Anchor Analogy
A stenographer inventing shorthand. Start with individual letters. Merge the most common letter pairs into a single symbol. Repeat until you have a compact vocabulary that covers most text efficiently — the rare words still spelled out letter by letter, the common ones compressed into a single stroke.
Why This Matters

Tokenisation is the first and last step of every LLM pipeline — it determines what the model sees and how it speaks. Tokens are not words. They are not characters. They are something in between, shaped by a statistical algorithm that ran on your training corpus. BPE quirks cause real production bugs: unexpected token counts that blow context windows, arithmetic that fails because numbers are split across multiple tokens, whitespace surprises that change model behaviour. Every one of those bugs only makes sense if you understand how the vocabulary was built.

Why Not Just Split on Spaces?

The obvious approach to splitting text into pieces is splitting on whitespace. "The cat sat" becomes ["The", "cat", "sat"]. Simple. Deterministic. Readable.

It falls apart immediately when you try to build a language model on it.

A real corpus contains millions of unique word forms. "run", "runs", "running", "runner", "ran" are five separate vocabulary entries. So are every misspelling, every hyphenated compound, every URL, every code snippet, every number in every format. If your vocabulary is the set of all words seen in training, you end up with millions of entries. New documents will always contain words you have never seen — out-of-vocabulary tokens that the model literally cannot represent.

Character-level tokenisation solves the vocabulary explosion: you have at most a few hundred unique characters, and you can always represent any text. But a sentence that takes 8 word-tokens now takes 40 character-tokens. The model must learn to attend across four times as many positions to understand the same content. Attention cost scales quadratically with sequence length. Character-level models are slow and struggle to capture long-range dependencies.

Byte-Pair Encoding, first adapted for neural machine translation by (Sennrich et al., 2016) and later the basis of GPT-2's tokeniser (Radford et al., 2019), sits between these extremes. It compresses common sequences into single tokens while preserving the ability to represent any text character by character as a fallback. The result: a fixed vocabulary of practical size, typically 30,000–100,000 tokens, that covers real text efficiently.

The BPE Algorithm Step by Step

BPE is a greedy compression algorithm. It starts with the smallest possible units and merges the most common pairs repeatedly until it reaches a target vocabulary size.

Here is the algorithm on a tiny corpus. Imagine your entire training set is four sentences:

"low low low low low"
"lowest lowest lowest"
"newer newer newer newer"
"wider wider wider"

Step 1: Initialize the vocabulary with characters

Split every word into its constituent characters. BPE typically adds a special end-of-word marker (often </w> or a space prefix) to distinguish "low" as a standalone word from "low" as a prefix in "lowest". For now, represent the word "low" as the character sequence l o w and count how often each full word appears.

l o w        : 5   (from "low" appearing 5 times)
l o w e s t  : 3   (from "lowest" appearing 3 times)
n e w e r    : 4   (from "newer" appearing 4 times)
w i d e r    : 3   (from "wider" appearing 3 times)

The initial vocabulary is the set of all unique characters: {l, o, w, e, s, t, n, r, i, d}. Ten entries.

Step 2: Count all adjacent character pairs

Look at every adjacent pair of characters across all word instances, weighted by how often that word appears:

('e', 'r')  : 4 + 3 = 7   (from "newer" x4, "wider" x3)
('l', 'o')  : 5 + 3 = 8   (from "low" x5, "lowest" x3)
('o', 'w')  : 5 + 3 = 8   (from "low" x5, "lowest" x3)
('e', 's')  : 3            (from "lowest" x3)
...

The most frequent pair is a tie between ('l', 'o') and ('o', 'w'), both at count 8.

Step 3: Merge the most frequent pair

Pick the winner (ties broken by position or alphabetically — let's take ('l', 'o')). Create a new token lo and replace every occurrence of the pair l o with the merged token:

lo w         : 5
lo w e s t   : 3
n e w e r    : 4
w i d e r    : 3

Vocabulary is now {l, o, w, e, s, t, n, r, i, d, lo} — eleven entries.

Step 4: Repeat until target vocabulary size

Count pairs again. Now ('lo', 'w') appears 8 times. Merge it to get low. Then ('e', 'r') appears 7 times. Merge it to get er. Continue until you have reached your target — say, 50 unique tokens. At that point you stop.

Each merge is recorded as a merge rule. The final tokeniser is simply a list of merge rules in the order they were learned, plus the base character vocabulary. To tokenise new text, you apply the rules from first to last.

Key Insight

BPE does not pick its vocabulary by any human intuition about words. It picks whatever byte sequences are statistically most compressible in the training corpus. Common English words get single tokens. Rare words get split into pieces that the model has seen in other contexts. Brand new words are always representable as character sequences — the fallback is built in.

How Tokenisation Works at Inference Time

You have the merge rule list. A new string arrives. How do you tokenise it?

Start by splitting the string into individual bytes or characters. Then scan the merge rules from first to last. At each rule, find every occurrence of that pair in your current sequence and merge them. After applying all rules, what remains is your token sequence.

Here is a worked example. Suppose the merge rules learned (in order) are:

Rule 1: l + o → lo
Rule 2: lo + w → low
Rule 3: e + r → er
Rule 4: low + er → lower

Tokenise the word "lower":

Start: character sequence

l o w e r

Apply Rule 1: l + o → lo

lo w e r

Apply Rule 2: lo + w → low

low e r

Apply Rule 3: e + r → er

low er

Apply Rule 4: low + er → lower

lower — a single token. The word was common enough in training that it earned its own entry.

Now tokenise "lowing" (a word the algorithm never saw):

Start:    l o w i n g
Rule 1:   lo w i n g
Rule 2:   low i n g
Rules 3-4: no match
Result:   low  i  n  g   (four tokens)

The model has never seen "lowing" as a unit, but it can represent it using pieces it understands. This is the graceful degradation that makes BPE practical.

Byte-Level BPE: GPT-2's Innovation

The tokeniser described above works on characters. GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) introduced an important variant: byte-level BPE. Instead of starting from Unicode characters, it starts from the 256 possible byte values.

Why bytes? Because Unicode has 140,000+ code points and multi-byte encodings. A character-level BPE must handle encoding failures when it encounters characters not in its base vocabulary. Bytes have no such failure mode. Every possible string can be expressed as a sequence of bytes. The fallback vocabulary is always complete.

In practice, GPT-2's tokeniser maps each byte to a printable Unicode character to avoid control characters in the vocabulary, then applies BPE on those representations. The mechanism is identical; only the base units differ.

GPT-4 uses a different tokeniser called cl100k_base (available via the tiktoken library), with a 100,277-token vocabulary. Llama models use SentencePiece with a similar BPE approach. The details differ, but the core algorithm is the same.

Why the Same Word Tokenises Differently in Different Contexts

This surprises many practitioners. How can the same string produce different tokens depending on what surrounds it?

The answer is whitespace. BPE tokenisers typically treat a leading space as part of a token. The token for " low" (space + l + o + w) is often a different token from "low" (no leading space). At the start of a sentence, "low" has no preceding space and may tokenise differently from "low" in the middle of a sentence where a space precedes it.

GPT-2's tokeniser specifically preserves whitespace in this way. The token " the" (space + the) is one of the most common tokens in the vocabulary. But "the" without a leading space — as it appears after a newline or at the start of a string — may tokenise differently, or use a separate vocabulary entry.

The practical consequence: if you are comparing two strings by counting tokens, stripping whitespace before tokenising can give you the wrong count. If you are prompting a model and you remove a leading space, the first word's token representation changes, which can subtly alter the model's probabilities for what comes next.

Think first: why does "1 + 1" sometimes produce 3 tokens and sometimes 5? +

Numbers are tokenised by BPE based on frequency in the training corpus. "1" may be its own token. "+" may be its own token. The space between them may or may not attach to the adjacent symbol depending on the merge rules. For multi-digit numbers like "128" or "1024", BPE may have learned to merge common digit sequences or may not, depending on how often those exact sequences appeared during training. This is why arithmetic can fail unexpectedly: "128" might tokenise as one token in some contexts and as "12" + "8" or "1" + "28" in others, depending on surrounding context and tokeniser version.

The Vocabulary Size Trade-off

Choosing the vocabulary size is an engineering decision with real consequences.

A small vocabulary (say, 8,000 tokens) means more merges are needed to represent any given text. Common words become single tokens, but even moderately uncommon ones get split. Sequences are longer. Attention cost rises. The model must do more work to assemble meaning from pieces.

A large vocabulary (say, 200,000 tokens) means more words get single-token representations. Sequences are shorter. But the embedding matrix grows: each vocabulary entry has a corresponding embedding vector. A vocabulary of 200,000 with 4,096-dimensional embeddings requires 200,000 × 4,096 = 819 million parameters just for the embedding layer. The output projection (mapping back from hidden dimension to vocabulary probabilities) has the same size. Memory and compute costs at the vocabulary layer become non-trivial.

Most production models settle in the 30,000–100,000 range as a practical compromise. GPT-2 used 50,257. GPT-4 uses 100,277. Llama 2 uses 32,000.

Active Debate

There is ongoing research into whether tokenisation is the right primitive for language models at all. Character-level and byte-level models that learn from the ground up — without any BPE merging — have shown competitive results at scale (Yu et al., 2023). The argument: letting the model learn its own compression, rather than applying a hand-designed statistical algorithm first, might produce better representations. The counter-argument: BPE compression dramatically reduces sequence lengths, which reduces training compute, which matters at scale. The debate is not settled. Most frontier models still use BPE or a close variant.

Consequences for Model Behaviour

Understanding BPE unlocks explanations for several model behaviours that otherwise seem mysterious.

Arithmetic failures

A model trained to add numbers faces an asymmetric task depending on how those numbers tokenise. "1 + 1" tokenises cleanly. "127 + 893" may tokenise as "12", "7", " +", " 8", "93" — five tokens with no alignment to the mathematical structure of the problem. The model must learn to do arithmetic on fragmented input, which is much harder. This partially explains why small models fail on multi-digit arithmetic and why chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) helps: writing intermediate steps externalises the carry operations into the token stream where the model can process them sequentially.

Non-English language degradation

BPE learns from the training corpus. If that corpus is 90% English, English words get compressed into single or double tokens while non-English text gets fragmented far more aggressively. A Chinese character that maps to a single UTF-8 byte sequence might take four tokens to represent. This means non-English input is effectively compressed less, sequences are longer, and the model has proportionally less context to work with per semantic unit. The degradation is not a model capacity problem — it is a tokenisation artefact.

Context window calculations

When you ask "how many words can fit in a 4,096-token context window", the answer is not 4,096. English text averages roughly 0.75 words per token, so 4,096 tokens is approximately 3,000 words of clean English prose. Dense technical text or code may be closer to 1:1. Non-English text may be worse. The only accurate way to know how many tokens your input uses is to run it through the tokeniser.

Prompt injection and adversarial inputs

Certain strings tokenise in unexpected ways, which can be exploited. Unusual Unicode characters, repeated punctuation, and strings that trigger unusual merge patterns can consume far more tokens than their visual length suggests, potentially overflowing context windows or triggering unexpected model behaviour. Understanding that the model operates on tokens, not characters or words, is the first step to reasoning about these vulnerabilities.

Implementing BPE in Python

Here is a minimal BPE implementation from scratch. This is not production-grade but illustrates every step of the algorithm.

from collections import Counter, defaultdict

def get_vocab(corpus):
    """Split corpus into character-level word frequency dict."""
    vocab = Counter()
    for word in corpus.split():
        vocab[' '.join(list(word)) + ' </w>'] += 1
    return vocab

def get_pairs(vocab):
    """Count all adjacent symbol pairs across the vocabulary."""
    pairs = defaultdict(int)
    for word, freq in vocab.items():
        symbols = word.split()
        for i in range(len(symbols) - 1):
            pairs[(symbols[i], symbols[i + 1])] += freq
    return pairs

def merge_vocab(pair, vocab):
    """Apply a single merge rule to the vocabulary."""
    merged = {}
    bigram = ' '.join(pair)
    replacement = ''.join(pair)
    for word, freq in vocab.items():
        new_word = word.replace(bigram, replacement)
        merged[new_word] = freq
    return merged

def train_bpe(corpus, num_merges):
    """Run BPE training for num_merges iterations."""
    vocab = get_vocab(corpus)
    merge_rules = []

    for i in range(num_merges):
        pairs = get_pairs(vocab)
        if not pairs:
            break
        best_pair = max(pairs, key=pairs.get)
        vocab = merge_vocab(best_pair, vocab)
        merge_rules.append(best_pair)
        print(f"Merge {i+1}: {best_pair[0]} + {best_pair[1]} "
              f"(count={pairs[best_pair]})")

    return vocab, merge_rules

# Example
corpus = "low low low lowest lowest newer newer newer newer wider wider wider"
vocab, rules = train_bpe(corpus, num_merges=10)

Running this produces the merge sequence described earlier. The vocabulary dictionary after training shows the compressed word forms. The merge rules list is what you save — it is the tokeniser.

Applying the rules to new text

def tokenise(word, merge_rules):
    """Apply merge rules to a single word."""
    symbols = list(word) + ['</w>']

    for pair in merge_rules:
        i = 0
        new_symbols = []
        while i < len(symbols):
            if (i < len(symbols) - 1 and
                    symbols[i] == pair[0] and
                    symbols[i + 1] == pair[1]):
                new_symbols.append(''.join(pair))
                i += 2
            else:
                new_symbols.append(symbols[i])
                i += 1
        symbols = new_symbols

    return symbols

print(tokenise('lower', rules))   # ['lower', '</w>'] if 'lower' is learned
print(tokenise('lowing', rules))  # ['low', 'i', 'n', 'g', '</w>']

The function iterates through merge rules in learned order, applying each one greedily. This greedy left-to-right application is important: it means the first applicable rule wins, which is why rule ordering matters.

Key Insight

Tokens are not words. They are the subword units that were statistically most useful to merge during training on a specific corpus. Every quirk of model behaviour that involves text format — number arithmetic, non-English degradation, whitespace sensitivity, context window calculations — traces back to this single fact. The model never sees your text; it sees a sequence of integers. Understanding how that conversion works is not optional background knowledge. It is the foundation of reliable prompt engineering and LLM debugging.
