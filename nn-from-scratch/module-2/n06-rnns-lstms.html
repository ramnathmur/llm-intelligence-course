RNNs and LSTMs — Sequence Modelling Before Transformers

The sequence models that came before transformers — and why vanishing gradients killed them.

~15 min

Before 2017, if you wanted a neural network to process language, time-series data, or any input where order mattered, there was one dominant answer: the Recurrent Neural Network, or RNN. It powered Google Translate, speech recognition, and early chatbots. It also had a fatal flaw that the field spent a decade trying to paper over.

This page tells the full story: how RNNs work, why they fail, how the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) cell partially rescued them, and why — despite those rescue efforts — the transformer still made both architectures obsolete. Understanding this history is not nostalgia. It is the fastest path to understanding why modern architectures are built the way they are.

The core problem: sequences are not fixed-length

Every model you built in earlier pages in this course takes a fixed-size input. Feed it a vector of 10 numbers, it produces an output. Feed it a vector of 11 numbers and it breaks — the weight matrices have the wrong shape.

Language does not work that way. "The cat sat." is three words. "The cat that the dog that the man owned chased sat." is twelve words. Both express something a model might need to process, and neither has a fixed length.

You could pad every sentence to the length of the longest one, but that wastes computation and obscures structure. You could use a sliding window, but then you lose dependencies between words that are far apart. The field needed an architecture that could process sequences of variable length, tracking information across positions.

The RNN was that architecture.

How an RNN works: the hidden state as a notepad

Imagine an analyst who must read a long strategy document and produce a summary. They cannot hold the whole document in their head at once. So they read one paragraph, jot down a few notes, then read the next paragraph with those notes in hand. The notes evolve as they read. By the end, their notepad captures whatever the process managed to retain.

An RNN works the same way. At each position in the sequence, it maintains a hidden state — a vector of numbers that acts as the notepad. When a new token (word, character, or data point) arrives, the RNN combines it with the current hidden state to produce a new hidden state. The updated state then carries forward to the next position.

Formally, at time step t, the RNN computes:

h_t = tanh(W_h @ h_(t-1) + W_x @ x_t + b)

Where h_t is the new hidden state, h_(t-1) is the previous hidden state, x_t is the current input token, W_h and W_x are learned weight matrices, and tanh is the activation function from N03 — Activation Functions.

Notice what is the same across all time steps: the weight matrices W_h and W_x. The RNN uses the same parameters at every position. This is called weight sharing across time, and it is what allows the same model to handle sequences of any length.

The output at each time step is typically derived from the hidden state:

y_t = W_out @ h_t + b_out

In a language model, y_t would be a probability distribution over the vocabulary — the model's prediction of what comes next.

Training an RNN: Backpropagation Through Time

Training an RNN uses the same principle as training any neural network: compute the loss, take the gradient, update the weights. But the computation graph for an RNN stretches across time. Each time step depends on the previous one, so the gradient must flow backwards through all the time steps to reach the weight matrices. This is called Backpropagation Through Time, or BPTT (Werbos, 1990).

Think of it like a post-project review at a consulting firm. The client engagement produced a bad outcome. To identify the root cause, the partners trace back through every decision in the project timeline — the final presentation, the interim report, the initial scoping, the first kick-off call. The responsibility for the bad outcome is distributed across all those decisions, weighted by how much each one contributed. BPTT does exactly this, tracing responsibility for a bad prediction back through every time step in the sequence.

The vanishing gradient problem

Here is where things go wrong. When you backpropagate through many time steps, the gradient must pass through the tanh activation at each step. The tanh function maps any input to a value between −1 and +1. Its derivative — the slope at any point — is always at most 1, and is often much smaller.

When you multiply many numbers that are each smaller than 1 together, the product shrinks toward zero. Fast.

If the gradient shrinks by a factor of 0.5 at each time step, then over 20 steps it becomes 0.5^20 = 0.000001. Over 100 steps, it is effectively zero. The weights that process early tokens receive gradients so small that they barely update at all. The model cannot learn from distant context.

Key Insight

The vanishing gradient problem is not a bug in the training algorithm. It is an inherent consequence of the RNN's architecture: multiplying the same weight matrix and the same bounded activation function together hundreds of times causes the gradient signal to exponentially decay. The network becomes deaf to information from far back in the sequence.

This is why the novel-reading analogy is so apt. Each time the RNN processes a new token, it is like rewriting the notepad with a slightly diluted version of its previous contents plus the new information. After 50 rewrites, what was written on page 1 of the notepad has been diluted into near-nothingness.

There is also a mirror-image problem: exploding gradients. If the weight matrix has values greater than 1, the gradient grows exponentially instead of shrinking, causing the weights to swing wildly during training. The standard fix is gradient clipping — if the gradient norm exceeds a threshold, scale it down. This patches the exploding problem but does nothing for the vanishing problem (Pascanu et al., 2013).

Think first: why does gradient vanishing specifically hurt long-range dependencies, not short-range ones? +

A gradient flowing back from time step 50 to time step 49 only travels one step and barely shrinks. A gradient flowing back from step 50 to step 1 must pass through 49 multiplications, each shrinking it. Short-range dependencies — like predicting the next word in "the cat sat on the ___" — require only a few steps of backpropagation. The gradient survives. Long-range dependencies — like resolving a pronoun to its subject 30 words earlier — require many steps. The gradient dies before it arrives. The model learns local patterns easily and global structure almost not at all.

The LSTM: gates as a solution

In 1997, Sepp Hochreiter and Jürgen Schmidhuber proposed a radical redesign: the Long Short-Term Memory cell (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). It became one of the most cited papers in machine learning history, because it worked.

The key insight: instead of trying to force gradients to survive through many tanh squashings, create a separate pathway — the cell state — where information can flow unchanged. Then use gates to control what flows into, out of, and through that pathway.

Think of a hospital patient's medical record. Three staff roles manage it:

  • A records clerk (forget gate) who reviews the current record and decides which old entries are no longer relevant.
  • An admitting nurse (input gate) who decides which new information from today's assessment gets added to the record.
  • A attending physician (output gate) who reads the record and decides what to share in the handover summary to the next team.

Each gate is a small neural network that outputs a vector of values between 0 and 1. Multiplying the cell state by values close to 0 erases information. Multiplying by values close to 1 preserves it. The model learns when to remember and when to forget, rather than having that determined by the fixed arithmetic of the architecture.

The four computations inside an LSTM cell

At each time step, the LSTM cell performs four computations. The inputs are: the previous hidden state h_(t-1), the previous cell state C_(t-1), and the current input x_t.

# Forget gate: what fraction of the old cell state to retain
f_t = sigmoid(W_f @ [h_(t-1), x_t] + b_f)

# Input gate: what new information to write
i_t = sigmoid(W_i @ [h_(t-1), x_t] + b_i)
C_tilde = tanh(W_C @ [h_(t-1), x_t] + b_C)

# Cell state update: combine old memory with new
C_t = f_t * C_(t-1) + i_t * C_tilde

# Output gate: what to read out as the hidden state
o_t = sigmoid(W_o @ [h_(t-1), x_t] + b_o)
h_t = o_t * tanh(C_t)

The notation [h_(t-1), x_t] means the two vectors are concatenated into one before the matrix multiply.

The key line is the cell state update: C_t = f_t * C_(t-1) + i_t * C_tilde. This is an additive update. The old cell state is scaled (possibly zeroed) and then added to new content. The gradient of this operation flows back through addition, which does not multiply the gradient by anything — it passes through intact. This is the LSTM's core trick: the cell state creates a gradient highway where information (and its gradients) can travel across many time steps without being destroyed by repeated multiplications (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997).

The Gated Recurrent Unit: a leaner variant

In 2014, Cho et al. proposed the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) (Cho et al., 2014). A GRU merges the cell state and hidden state into one, and replaces the three gates with two: a reset gate and an update gate. Fewer parameters, slightly simpler to train, and on many tasks essentially equivalent to an LSTM. Both became standard tools before the transformer era.

What LSTMs cannot fix

LSTMs improved greatly on vanilla RNNs. For sequences of a few hundred tokens, they could capture meaningful long-range dependencies. But they did not solve the problem — they deferred it.

Long-range memory is still limited

The cell state is not infinitely expressive. It is a fixed-size vector — perhaps 512 numbers — that must somehow compress the entire history of a sequence. For a novel-length input, that vector must decide what to keep and what to discard from thousands of prior tokens. The forget gate helps, but it is learned and imperfect. On sequences of thousands of tokens, LSTMs still lose track of distant information.

Bahdanau et al. (2015) partially fixed this for translation with the attention mechanism (Bahdanau et al., 2015) — letting the decoder look back at all encoder hidden states rather than just the final one. This was the seed idea that, two years later, became the transformer. But attention was bolted onto the LSTM, not built into it. The fundamental architecture was still recurrent.

Parallelisation is impossible

This is the LSTM's structural, unfixable problem.

Consider how you train an MLP. You have a batch of 256 examples. You compute the forward pass for all 256 simultaneously — one giant matrix multiplication. Modern GPUs are designed for exactly this: thousands of cores all doing matrix arithmetic in parallel.

Now consider training an LSTM on a sequence of 500 tokens. Step 2 depends on the hidden state from step 1. Step 3 depends on step 2. Step 500 depends on step 499. Each step must wait for the previous one to finish. You cannot run all 500 steps in parallel, because their outputs are causally chained. You are forced to process them sequentially, one step at a time.

This makes LSTM training slow. Not because the individual computations are expensive, but because a GPU designed to run 4,000 operations in parallel is being used to run 500 operations in strict sequence. You are wasting the hardware.

For a 500-token sequence with 512-dimensional hidden states, the GPU utilisation is a fraction of what it could be. For a 2,000-token sequence — a fairly short document by real-world standards — the problem compounds further. Training modern large-scale models on LSTMs would take not weeks but years.

A side-by-side comparison: RNN, LSTM, Transformer

Understanding the differences concretely prepares you for the transformer pages ahead.

RNN / LSTM
Transformer

Limited: information decays through time
Direct: every token attends to every other

None: must process tokens sequentially

Note the last row. During training, transformers win decisively on parallelism because the target sequence is known in advance and all positions can be processed at once. During inference (generating new tokens one at a time), transformers are also sequential — each new token depends on all previous ones. The parallelism advantage is primarily a training-time property, and it is the reason transformers could scale to sizes that would have been infeasible with LSTMs.

Why LSTMs still appear in some codebases

LSTMs are not extinct. For several problems, they remain competitive or preferable:

  • Very short sequences with tight latency constraints. On a 10-step time series, there is no meaningful long-range dependency to capture. An LSTM is simpler, faster to train, and easier to deploy on edge hardware than a full transformer.
  • Streaming data where you process one step at a time. If new data arrives continuously and you cannot buffer a full sequence, the recurrent architecture fits naturally. You keep the hidden state and update it with each new point.
  • Some audio and signal processing tasks. Convolutional-recurrent hybrids persist in speech recognition pipelines where the input is a short-window spectrogram frame sequence.

But for language, document understanding, code generation, or any task where sequences exceed a few hundred tokens and training data is plentiful, the transformer dominates. The LSTM's era as the default sequence model lasted from roughly 1997 to 2017 — two decades of productive use before a better design displaced it.

Think Further

LSTM gates are learned by the same gradient descent that trains the rest of the network. Early in training, the gates are random. What do you think the forget gate does before training converges — does it tend to forget more or retain more? And why might initialising the forget gate bias toward 1 (retain everything) early in training help gradient flow? This initialisation trick, proposed by Jozefowicz et al. (2015), became a standard practice.

The bridge to transformers

The transformer, proposed by Vaswani et al. in 2017, did not invent a new problem. It solved the two problems that RNNs and LSTMs could not:

Problem 1: Long-range dependencies. Instead of routing information through a chain of hidden states, transformers use self-attention to allow every token to directly attend to every other token. The path length between any two positions is always 1 step. No vanishing gradient across positions.

Problem 2: Parallelism. Because self-attention computes all pairwise relationships in one matrix operation (when the full target sequence is available), all positions can be processed simultaneously during training. The sequential bottleneck disappears.

When you later study self-attention, every design decision will make more sense with this background: why the query-key-value structure exists, why positional encodings are needed (self-attention has no built-in sense of order — a problem RNNs never had), and why the architecture exploded in scale as soon as it could be trained on GPUs at full utilisation.

The LSTM was not a dead end. It was the clearest possible proof of what the field needed next.
