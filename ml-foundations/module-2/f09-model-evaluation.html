Evaluation Metrics

Precision, recall, AUC-ROC — the scoreboard every model is judged against.

~15 min
Anchor Analogy
A hospital hiring a radiologist. Accuracy alone is useless — you need to know how often they catch disease (recall) versus how often they raise false alarms (precision). The tradeoff between those two numbers is the real hiring decision.
Why This Matters
Choosing the wrong evaluation metric produces a model that optimises for the wrong thing. A fraud detector evaluated on accuracy will learn to ignore fraud entirely if 99% of transactions are legitimate — and score 99% while doing it. Metrics are the contract between what you measure and what you actually care about. Getting that contract wrong wastes months of engineering effort and deploys systems that fail silently in production.
After This Page, You Can Argue
Which evaluation metric fits which problem type and why accuracy alone misleads in most real-world cases. You will be able to explain precision, recall, F1, and AUC-ROC in plain language, choose between them for a given business problem, and recognise when a high accuracy score is hiding a broken model.
Prerequisites: F04 — Train / Validation / Test Splits

When a model finishes training, you need a number to judge it by. That number is an evaluation metric. The choice of metric is not a technical detail you hand off to an engineer — it is a strategic decision about what your model is for. Pick the wrong metric and you will build exactly the model you measured, which may be exactly the model you do not want.

This page builds up the evaluation vocabulary from scratch: from the confusion matrix through precision and recall to F1 and AUC-ROC. Each concept connects to a business scenario where the wrong metric choice would have real consequences.

The confusion matrix: four ways to be right or wrong

All classification metrics trace back to a single table called the confusion matrix. Before diving into formulas, you need to be fluent in its four cells.

Suppose your model classifies loan applications as "approve" or "decline." Every prediction falls into one of four buckets:

  • True Positive (TP): The model predicted "approve" and the applicant was genuinely creditworthy. Correct, in the right direction.
  • True Negative (TN): The model predicted "decline" and the applicant would have defaulted. Correct, in the right direction.
  • False Positive (FP): The model predicted "approve" but the applicant defaulted. A costly mistake.
  • False Negative (FN): The model predicted "decline" but the applicant was actually creditworthy. A missed opportunity — and a potentially discriminatory outcome.

Every metric is just a different way of weighting these four numbers. The disagreement among metrics reflects genuine disagreement about which type of error costs more.

The confusion matrix. Every classification metric is a ratio computed from these four cells. The colour coding is intentional: green for the desirable outcome, amber for one type of error, red for the other, blue for the correct rejection.

Accuracy: the metric that sounds right but usually is not

Accuracy is the fraction of all predictions that were correct:

Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)

This seems sensible until you consider class imbalance. Suppose you are building a model to detect fraudulent transactions. In your dataset, 99 out of every 100 transactions are legitimate. A model that predicts "not fraud" for every single transaction, never once raising an alert, achieves 99% accuracy. But it catches zero fraud. Its accuracy is high and its utility is zero.

This is the accuracy paradox: in imbalanced datasets — which describe the vast majority of real business problems — accuracy rewards the model for correctly identifying the majority class while ignoring failure on the minority class. Fraud detection, medical diagnosis, equipment fault detection, rare event prediction — all suffer from this.

The Accuracy Trap
Any dataset where one class appears far more often than another is imbalanced. In such datasets, accuracy is almost always misleading. Suspect accuracy as your primary metric any time one of your classes represents less than 20% of the data — and certainly when it is less than 5%.

Precision: how trustworthy are your positive predictions?

Precision answers: "Of all the cases where the model said yes, what fraction actually were yes?"

Precision = TP / (TP + FP)

Think of a radiologist who flags suspicious lesions for biopsy. High precision means that when they flag something, it is almost always actually a lesion. Low precision means the biopsy team receives a stream of false alarms — costly, traumatic for patients, and erosive of trust in the radiologist's judgment.

Precision punishes false positives. If your cost structure is "false alarms are very expensive," optimise for precision.

In a spam filter context: high precision means the spam folder contains almost exclusively real spam. The occasional piece of real spam still slips to the inbox (false negatives), but the user's inbox is clean. Low precision means legitimate emails are being deleted — unacceptable for a business.

Recall: how many actual positives did you catch?

Recall answers: "Of all the actual positive cases, what fraction did the model find?"

Recall = TP / (TP + FN)

Back to the radiologist. High recall means they catch almost all the lesions that exist. Low recall means they are missing actual cancers — the most dangerous failure mode in clinical screening.

Recall punishes false negatives. If your cost structure is "missing a positive case is very expensive," optimise for recall.

In a fraud detection context: high recall means almost all fraudulent transactions get flagged. Some legitimate transactions may also get flagged (false positives), but the fraud team can review those. Missing actual fraud goes undetected entirely — a direct financial loss.

The precision-recall tradeoff

Precision and recall are not independent. They are in tension. Pulling one up typically pulls the other down.

Every classification model produces a score between 0 and 1 for each example. You choose a threshold: examples above the threshold are classified as positive, examples below as negative. This threshold is a dial.

  • Raise the threshold (only flag things you are very confident about): precision rises, recall falls. Fewer false alarms, but you start missing real positives.
  • Lower the threshold (flag anything remotely suspicious): recall rises, precision falls. You catch more real positives, but false alarms multiply.

The right threshold is not a mathematical question — it is a business decision about the relative cost of false positives versus false negatives. There is no universal correct answer. A cancer screening tool should prioritise recall over precision (missing a cancer is catastrophic; further testing resolves false alarms). A spam filter should prioritise precision over recall (deleting a real email is catastrophic; seeing occasional spam is tolerable).

Think first: You are building a model to predict equipment failure in a nuclear plant. Should you optimise for precision or recall? +

Recall. Missing an actual equipment failure (a false negative) could be catastrophic. You accept false alarms — a technician checks the equipment unnecessarily — because the cost of a missed failure far exceeds the cost of a spurious inspection. A false positive means wasted maintenance time. A false negative could mean a meltdown. When asymmetry of error costs is this extreme, recall dominates the decision.

F1 score: one number when you need to balance both

Sometimes you genuinely need to optimise for both precision and recall simultaneously. You cannot afford many false alarms and you cannot afford many missed positives. In that case, a single metric that combines them is useful.

The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall:

F1 = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

The harmonic mean has a useful property: it is pulled strongly toward the lower of the two values. A model with precision 0.9 and recall 0.1 has an arithmetic mean of 0.5 but an F1 of only 0.18. The F1 punishes imbalanced precision-recall pairs. A high F1 requires both to be high.

F1 is particularly useful when classes are imbalanced. It ignores true negatives entirely — which is exactly right for imbalanced problems, because a model that correctly ignores the majority class should not get credit for it.

Key Insight

F1 is not always the right answer just because classes are imbalanced. It treats precision and recall as equally important. If your business context actually makes false positives ten times more costly than false negatives (or vice versa), the weighted F-beta score lets you express that ratio explicitly: Fβ = (1+β²) × (Precision × Recall) / (β² × Precision + Recall). With β = 2 you weight recall twice as heavily as precision; with β = 0.5 you weight precision twice as heavily. Always ask "what is the cost ratio of our two error types?" before defaulting to F1.

AUC-ROC: evaluating the model across all thresholds

Every metric discussed so far depends on a threshold. But how good is the model's underlying discriminative ability, independent of any particular threshold choice?

The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve answers this. It plots:

  • True Positive Rate (Recall) on the y-axis: what fraction of real positives did we catch?
  • False Positive Rate on the x-axis: what fraction of real negatives did we mistakenly flag?

As you lower the threshold from 1.0 to 0.0, both rates increase. The curve traces that trajectory. A perfect classifier has a curve that goes straight up to (0, 1) and then straight right to (1, 1). A completely random classifier produces a diagonal straight line from (0, 0) to (1, 1).

The AUC (Area Under the Curve) summarises the entire curve as a single number between 0 and 1:

  • AUC = 1.0: Perfect classifier. Every positive is ranked above every negative.
  • AUC = 0.5: Random classifier. The model has no discriminative power — its predictions are coin flips.
  • AUC below 0.5: Consistently wrong. Rare in practice but indicates something has gone wrong, often a label encoding error.

ROC curves for three classifier types. The shaded area under the blue curve is the AUC. The ideal model hugs the top-left corner. A random model follows the diagonal. Most useful classifiers land somewhere in between.

AUC has a satisfying probabilistic interpretation: it equals the probability that the model ranks a randomly chosen positive example higher than a randomly chosen negative example (Fawcett, 2006). An AUC of 0.85 means that if you pick a random fraud case and a random legitimate case, there is an 85% chance the model assigns a higher fraud score to the fraud case.

AUC is particularly valuable when:

  • You want to compare two models without committing to a threshold yet
  • The threshold will be chosen later based on operational constraints
  • You are doing initial model selection during development

AUC-PR: the better choice for severe class imbalance

AUC-ROC has a weakness. In datasets with severe class imbalance, the false positive rate can look very favourable simply because there are so many negatives. The curve may look excellent while the model is still performing poorly on the rare positive class.

The Precision-Recall AUC (AUC-PR) plots precision on the y-axis and recall on the x-axis, sweeping across thresholds. It focuses entirely on how well the model handles positive examples, ignoring true negatives. For problems where the minority class is the focus — fraud, rare disease, equipment failure — AUC-PR is a more honest measure (Davis & Goadrich, 2006).

A random classifier on a 1% positive rate dataset has an AUC-ROC of 0.5 but an AUC-PR of only 0.01. AUC-PR makes the baseline performance visible.

Metrics for regression problems

Everything above applies to classification: the model predicts a category. When the model predicts a continuous number — house price, energy consumption, wait time — you need regression metrics.

Mean Absolute Error (MAE): The average of the absolute differences between predictions and actual values. Easy to interpret: "our model's predictions are off by £3,200 on average." Not punished by the occasional catastrophic miss.

Mean Squared Error (MSE): The average of the squared differences. Squaring means large errors are penalised disproportionately. A model that is occasionally very wrong will have a much worse MSE than one that is consistently slightly wrong, even if the MAE is similar. Use MSE when catastrophic misses matter more than routine errors.

Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): The square root of MSE, bringing the error back into the same units as the original prediction. More interpretable than MSE, still penalises large errors. The most commonly reported regression metric in practice.

R² (R-squared): Measures what fraction of the variance in the target variable the model explains. An R² of 0.8 means the model explains 80% of why the target variable varies across examples. R² of 1.0 is a perfect fit; R² of 0 means the model is no better than predicting the mean every time.

Matching metrics to business problems

The single most useful table in applied machine learning is one that maps problem types to metric choices.

Balanced classes, equal error costs: Accuracy is acceptable. Both errors are equally bad, the classes appear in similar proportions.

False positives costlier than false negatives: Optimise for Precision. Examples: legal document flagging (wrongly flagging a clean document has compliance cost), recommendation systems (irrelevant recommendations erode trust).

False negatives costlier than false positives: Optimise for Recall. Examples: disease screening (a missed cancer is catastrophic), safety monitoring (a missed fault can cause injury).

Both errors matter, you need one number: Use F1 score (equal cost) or Fβ score (explicitly weighted cost ratio).

Comparing models before threshold selection: Use AUC-ROC. It ranks the models independently of the operating threshold.

Every error matters equally: Use MAE. Easy to interpret in real-world units. A delivery-time prediction model might report "average error of 4.2 minutes."

Outliers matter more than average: Use RMSE or MSE. A financial forecasting model where a single large miss is catastrophic should prioritise minimising RMSE.

You need a scale-free comparison: Use R². Comparing models across different targets (price in GBP vs. delivery time in minutes) requires a normalised measure.

AUC-ROC limitation: With severe imbalance (e.g., 0.1% positive rate), AUC-ROC can look good while the model fails on the rare class. A false positive rate of 0.01 sounds excellent until you realise it still produces 1,000 false alarms per 100,000 negatives.

Use AUC-PR instead: The Precision-Recall curve stays focused on the rare class. A random classifier shows an AUC-PR equal to the positive rate — for a 1% positive rate, that is 0.01. A good classifier should be substantially above that baseline.

F1 as threshold-specific summary: Once you have chosen an operating threshold, F1 summarises performance on the minority class without counting true negatives. It will not inflate due to the model correctly rejecting majority-class examples.

Calibration: when probabilities need to be trusted

Some use cases do not just need a classification decision — they need the model's confidence to be reliable. A model that assigns a 70% fraud probability should be right about 70% of the time, not 40% or 90%.

This property is called calibration. A well-calibrated model's predicted probabilities match observed frequencies (Platt, 1999). AUC and F1 measure discrimination (can the model rank positives above negatives?) but not calibration (are the probability estimates trustworthy?). These are separate properties. A model can rank examples perfectly but have terrible calibration.

Calibration matters when downstream decisions depend on the probability itself: insurance pricing, credit risk scoring, clinical decision support, probabilistic capacity planning. In these contexts, add a calibration plot (reliability diagram) to your evaluation toolkit.

Putting it all together: an evaluation workflow

When you face a new model evaluation task, work through these questions in order:

Step 1: What type of problem is this? Classification or regression? That immediately narrows your metric universe.

Step 2: Is the dataset balanced? If one class is less than 10% of the data, remove accuracy from consideration for classification. Move to precision, recall, F1, or AUC-PR depending on your error cost structure.

Step 3: What is the cost asymmetry? Are false positives or false negatives more damaging for the business? Articulate this as a ratio (e.g., "a missed fraud case costs us 10x more than a false alarm that requires a manual review"). That ratio guides whether you optimise for precision, recall, or a weighted F-beta.

Step 4: Do you need probabilities or just decisions? If downstream systems consume the probability directly — pricing models, risk scorecards — evaluate calibration separately from discrimination.

Step 5: Choose a primary metric for model selection and one or two guardrail metrics. A fraud model might use AUC-PR as primary, with precision ≥ 0.8 as a guardrail to ensure the human review queue does not overflow.

This framework also applies when evaluating work produced by others. When a vendor claims their model achieves 98% accuracy, the first question is always: on what class distribution, against what baseline, and with what threshold? The answer almost always reveals something important.

Think Further

Large language models are evaluated on completely different metrics: perplexity, BLEU score, ROUGE, human preference ratings (win rates), and task-specific benchmarks like MMLU. These reflect a fundamentally different problem structure — there is no single "correct" output for most LLM tasks, so binary classification metrics do not apply. As you encounter these in the AI autonomy course, you will recognise the same underlying logic: every metric is a specific bet about what the model should optimise for, and that bet is always open to question.

Key Insight

The choice of evaluation metric is a statement about values, not just mathematics. When you choose recall over precision, you are saying "missing a real case is worse than a false alarm." When you choose accuracy, you are implicitly saying "all classes and all errors are equally important" — which is almost never true in practice. Evaluation metric choice is one of the most consequential decisions in a machine learning project, and it should be made by the person who understands the business consequences of each type of error, not defaulted to the software library's built-in score.
