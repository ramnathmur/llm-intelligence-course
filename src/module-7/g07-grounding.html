The Grounding Question

Does an LLM understand meaning or manipulate symbols without understanding? The honest answer is somewhere in between, with evidence on both sides.

~15 min
Anchor Analogy
The Chinese Room — symbol manipulation at the component level vs. possible understanding at the system level.
Why This Matters
Distinguishing "stochastic parrot" from "genuine understanding." The truth is a defensible middle ground.
After This Page, You Can Argue
After this page, you can hold the sophisticated middle position: "It's not a parrot and it's not human understanding — it's something new, with measurable evidence on both sides."
Prerequisites: G1 — What Weights Actually Encode G5 — Emergent Capabilities G26 — Mechanistic Interpretability

Picture a person locked in a sealed room. Slips of paper covered in Chinese characters slide in under the door. The person speaks no Chinese — the symbols are meaningless squiggles to them. But they have a fat rulebook in English: "if you see this shape followed by that shape, write down these shapes and slide them back out." They follow the rules, character by character, and push slips back under the door. To the Chinese speakers outside, the answers are fluent, intelligent, even witty. Inside, the person understands nothing. They are shuffling symbols by their shape, never their meaning.

That is John Searle's Chinese Room (Searle, 1980), and it is the sharpest version of the worry that hangs over this entire course. An LLM, the argument goes, is the person in the room. It manipulates tokens by statistical shape — which symbol tends to follow which — without ever touching what those symbols are about. It produces fluent French without knowing what France is, writes about grief without ever having felt loss. The technical name for what it would be missing is grounding: a connection between the symbols and the things in the world the symbols refer to. This page is about whether that connection exists, and the honest answer is messier and more interesting than either camp wants it to be.

Callback: how does the Othello world-model result act as evidence for "genuine semantics" — and what does the stochastic-parrot camp reply?+

In G34 — Internal World Models you saw Li et al. (2023) train a model on nothing but strings of Othello moves — "E3, D3, C5…" — with no picture of a board, no rules, no notion that a board even exists. Probing the trained network's activations, they recovered a clean internal map of the 8×8 board state, and editing that internal map changed the model's next-move predictions in exactly the way flipping a real disc would. The pro-semantics reading: the model didn't just learn which move-string follows which: it built a representation of the thing the symbols are about — the board — from the symbols alone. That looks like grounding emerging from pure sequence prediction. The stochastic-parrot reply: Othello is a closed, fully-specified game world where the "meaning" of every token is exhausted by the rules; recovering board state is impressive compression, but it is not evidence the model grasps anything outside that toy system — least of all the open, embodied, social meanings that words like "pain" or "justice" carry. Both readings are defensible, and that tension is exactly what this page is about.

What "grounding" actually means

Strip the jargon and grounding is a simple idea: a symbol is grounded when it is connected to the thing it stands for. When you read the word "apple," it links to a web of real experience — the taste, the weight in your hand, the crunch, the times you packed one in a lunchbox. The word is a handle on something you have actually encountered. The classic worry, sharpened by Stevan Harnad as the "symbol grounding problem," is that a system trained only on text has handles connected to nothing but other handles. "Apple" is defined by "fruit," "fruit" by "food," "food" by "eat," and you can chase the definitions in circles forever without any of them ever touching an actual apple. It is, Harnad said, like trying to learn Chinese from a Chinese-to-Chinese dictionary: every word is explained using more words you also don't know.

So the grounding question, stated plainly, is this: does an LLM understand what its words mean, or does it only know how words relate to other words? The first is semantics — meaning. The second is syntax — structure and shape. Searle's whole argument is that you can have unlimited syntax and zero semantics, and the Chinese Room is the proof: perfect symbol-shuffling, perfect output, nobody home.

The case for "stochastic parrot"

The most influential statement of the skeptical position comes from Bender and colleagues in a paper whose title became a slogan: "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots" (Bender et al., 2021). Their definition is precise and worth quoting in spirit: a language model is "a system for haphazardly stitching together sequences of linguistic forms it has observed in its vast training data, according to probabilistic information about how they combine, but without any reference to meaning." A parrot that has heard enough English to produce convincing strings — but a parrot, not a speaker.

The argument rests on a genuinely important distinction the authors draw between form and meaning. Form is the observable shape of language — the marks on the page, the sounds, the tokens. Meaning is the relation between those forms and something outside language: intentions, referents, communicative goals. Their claim is that a model trained purely on form has access to form only. It can learn, with stunning fidelity, the statistics of how forms co-occur. But co-occurrence is not reference. The model has never stood in the relation to the world that gives a word its meaning — it has only ever seen the word sitting next to other words. On this view, what looks like understanding is a kind of pareidolia: we supply the meaning when we read the fluent output, the way we see a face in a cloud. The model supplies only the form.

This is not a fringe complaint, and it would be a mistake to caricature it. The stochastic-parrot camp has the upper hand on several concrete points. Models hallucinate — they assert false things with total fluency, which is exactly what you would expect from a system optimizing for plausible form rather than tracking truth. They can be brittle in ways no grounded understander would be, failing on a problem after a trivial rewording that preserves the meaning but changes the surface form. And there is a deep point underneath: a system trained only to predict the next token has, by construction, no built-in pressure to care whether its outputs are true, only whether they are likely. Likelihood and truth usually travel together in good training data, but they are not the same thing, and the gap between them is where the parrot lives.

The case for genuine semantics

Now the other side, which has gotten substantially stronger in the years since the parrot paper. The strongest evidence is not philosophical but empirical, and you have already met it: the world-model results from G34. The Othello experiment (Li et al., 2023) matters here precisely because it attacks the parrot's central assumption. The parrot argument says a system trained on form has access to form only. The Othello model was trained on form only — bare move sequences — and yet it built an internal representation of something the forms refer to: the board. That is the symbol grounding problem being solved, in miniature, from the inside. The symbols ("E3") turned out to be grounded in a learned model of the thing they denote (a square on a board). Not because anyone connected them, but because predicting the next move well requires tracking the underlying state.

This dovetails with what you saw two modules back. In G1 — What Weights Actually Encode, the point was that the weights don't store sentences; they store compressed structure. And G26 — Mechanistic Interpretability showed that this structure can be read out: researchers have found directions in a model's activation space that correspond to concepts, features that activate for "the Golden Gate Bridge" whether the input is a photo, the words, or an oblique reference. A feature that fires for a concept across wildly different surface forms is doing something a pure form-tracker could not: it has abstracted away from the form to the thing the forms are about. That is the signature of meaning, not shape.

Self-Check
Why does the Othello world-model result count as evidence against the strong stochastic-parrot claim — and not merely as impressive pattern-matching?

There is also a behavioral case, weaker than the mechanistic one but not nothing. Models generalize to genuinely novel combinations — problems and phrasings that cannot have appeared verbatim in training (you saw this pressure in G5 — Emergent Capabilities). A pure lookup table of forms degrades gracelessly off its training distribution; these systems often degrade gracefully, or don't degrade at all, which suggests they have captured a relation more abstract than surface co-occurrence. None of this proves human-style understanding. But it makes the flat claim "no reference to meaning whatsoever" hard to sustain.

The Chinese Room, steelmanned on both sides

The deepest move in this debate is one Searle anticipated and most people miss. His Chinese Room has a famous reply, and the clash between them is the cleanest way to see why the grounding question is hard. The interactive below lays out the strongest version of each side. Don't skip to a verdict — sit in the tension, because that tension is the actual state of the science.

Steelman the skeptic, then the believer: where does understanding live — in the component or the system?+

The skeptic's strongest case (Searle's original). The person in the room follows the rulebook perfectly and understands not one word of Chinese. That is just a fact about the person — no amount of faster shuffling, bigger rulebooks, or more practice will make Chinese mean anything to them. Now notice: an LLM is doing exactly this. Each layer of a transformer is a deterministic arithmetic operation on numbers — matrix multiplications, no different in kind from the rule-following in the room. If you trace any single component, you find pure syntax: shapes in, shapes out, no meaning anywhere. And here is the kicker: you can't get semantics by stacking more syntax. A billion meaningless operations composed together are still meaningless operations. Understanding can't emerge from a process that, examined at every level, contains none of it.

The believer's strongest case (the "Systems Reply"). Searle is looking in the wrong place. Of course the person doesn't understand Chinese — but the person is just one part. The understander, if there is one, is the whole system: person plus rulebook plus the vast organized memory plus the process running over all of it. Asking whether the person understands Chinese is like asking whether a single neuron in your brain understands English. It doesn't — no individual neuron does — yet the system of 86 billion of them clearly does. Understanding is a property of the organized whole, not of any component, and "no component understands, therefore the system doesn't" is precisely the fallacy of composition. Searle's reply — "let the person memorize the entire rulebook, internalize the whole system, and they still won't understand Chinese" — sounds decisive but quietly assumes what it's trying to prove: that there is nothing it is like to be the running system, that the only candidate understander is the person's ordinary conscious self. A functionalist (you'll meet this view in G10 and especially G31 — Intentionality and Aboutness) says: if the system reliably produces the right relations between inputs, internal states, and outputs — and the world-model evidence says it does — then meaning is exactly what those relations are. There is no further fact about "real" understanding hiding behind the functional organization.

Why neither side wins cleanly. The skeptic is right that you can't point to meaning in any component. The believer is right that you can't infer the absence of meaning in a system from its absence in the parts. Both intuitions are powerful and they collide head-on. The honest reader holds both at once rather than pretending one obviously dissolves the other.

What makes the room argument so durable is that it isolates a real gap. Even granting the Systems Reply, there is a difference between a system whose internal states track the world (which the Othello result demonstrates) and a system whose internal states are about the world in the full-blooded way a human thought is about its object. The first is a measurable, mechanistic claim. The second — intentionality — is a philosophical one this course handles separately in G31. Conflating them is the single most common mistake in this debate, made by hype and dismissal alike.

The defensible middle: partial grounding

So where does an honest practitioner land? Not on either pole. The position the evidence supports — and the one you can defend in any debate — is partial grounding. Lay the two camps side by side and the middle becomes visible.

Stochastic Parrot (pure syntax)

"It stitches together forms by probability with no reference to meaning. What looks like understanding is us projecting meaning onto fluent output. Strongest evidence: hallucination, brittleness, and the fact that nothing in the training objective rewards truth over plausibility."

Partial Grounding (the defensible middle)

"It has built real internal models of some things its symbols refer to — demonstrably so for closed worlds like Othello, and partially for concepts that recur across forms. But that grounding is incomplete, indirect (learned from text about the world, not the world), and silent on consciousness or full intentionality. It is neither a parrot nor a human understander. It is a new thing."

What does "partial" cut along? Three honest limits. First, the grounding is second-hand: the model learned about apples from billions of sentences about apples, never from an apple. That is a real handicap for anything where direct sensory or motor experience does the heavy lifting — which is why multimodal training, feeding the model images and audio alongside text, measurably improves grounding for exactly those concepts. Second, it is uneven: grounding looks robust for well-structured domains with abundant data (board games, code, common physical facts) and thin for rare, ambiguous, or experience-laden concepts. Third, and most important, grounding is not consciousness. A system can have internal states that genuinely refer to the world while there is nothing it is like to be that system. Tracking the world and feeling the world are different claims, and the evidence for the first says nothing about the second — a separation this course makes its whole next page about, in G10 — Intelligence vs. Consciousness.

This is why the question "is it a stochastic parrot?" has a defensible answer you can actually stand behind in a debate, and it is neither "yes" nor "no." It is: the strong parrot claim — no reference to meaning at all — is contradicted by direct evidence that models build manipulable internal representations of what their symbols denote. But the strong understanding claim — it means things the way you and I do — outruns the evidence too. The truth is partial, asymmetric, second-hand grounding: real semantics, of a kind we have never seen before, in a system we are still learning to describe. That is not a hedge. It is the most precise thing the current science licenses, and saying it confidently — while flagging what remains genuinely open — is exactly what separates an informed position from a slogan in either direction.

Researchers do still disagree, and you should hold this lightly. Some read the world-model results as decisive against the parrot; others insist that recovering board state is sophisticated compression, not meaning, and that "grounding" smuggles in more than the data shows. The disagreement is real and the question is open. What is not seriously contested anymore is the flat 2021 claim that there is no reference to meaning whatsoever — the mechanistic evidence has moved the debate off that pole and onto the harder, more honest ground of how much and what kind.

Key Insight

The grounding debate is not a coin flip between "parrot" and "understander." The defensible position is partial grounding: models demonstrably build internal representations of what their symbols refer to (the Othello board, concepts that recur across forms), so the strong claim "no reference to meaning at all" is contradicted by evidence — but that grounding is second-hand, uneven, and entirely silent on consciousness, so the strong claim "it understands like a human" outruns the evidence too. It is a genuinely new kind of meaning, and the honest move is to say so with precision rather than collapsing to either slogan.
