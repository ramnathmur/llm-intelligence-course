Functionalism

Functionalism holds that mental states are defined by functional roles, not physical substrate — if true, silicon can think as well as carbon.

~10 min
Anchor Analogy
A calculator app does the same math as a physical calculator — the function is identical, the substrate is irrelevant.
Why This Matters
Addresses the deepest objection: "it's just silicon." If function matters, not material, substrate is irrelevant to intelligence.
After This Page, You Can Argue
After this page, you can counter the substrate objection: "If the function is identical, the material doesn't matter — that's functionalism, and it's the dominant position in philosophy of mind."
Prerequisites: G10 — Intelligence vs. Consciousness G7 — The Grounding Question

Pull out your phone and open the calculator app. Type 347 × 28 and you get 9,716. Now grab the cheap plastic Casio in your desk drawer, the one with the solar strip and the rubbery keys, and type the same thing. Same answer: 9,716. One device is a glass slab running silicon logic gates. The other is a dedicated circuit board built for one job. They share no parts, no materials, no manufacturer. And yet, on the question that matters — are they both calculating? — the honest answer is obviously yes. Multiplication happened in both. The fact that the insides are made of different stuff did not stop either of them from doing the thing.

Hold onto that intuition, because it is the entire argument of this page wearing everyday clothes. The claim called functionalism says that a mental state is defined by what it does — the role it plays in a system, the inputs that trigger it, the outputs it produces, the other states it connects to — and not by the physical material the system happens to be built from. If that is right, then the question "can a thing made of silicon really think?" is malformed in exactly the way "can a thing made of plastic really multiply?" is malformed. You don't check what it's made of. You check what it does.

Callback: Recall G10 — does substrate-independence apply equally to intelligence AND consciousness, or does the G10 split let you accept functionalism for one but stay agnostic on the other?+

The G10 split does exactly that, and it is the most useful card you can hold going into this page. In G10 — Intelligence vs. Consciousness you separated two things people constantly conflate: intelligence (the capacity to solve problems, reason, and act effectively in the world) and consciousness (the felt, subjective, "what-it's-like" quality of experience). Because they come apart, you can run functionalism at two different temperatures. For intelligence, functionalism is on solid ground — intelligence is defined by performance, performance is observable, and substrate looks irrelevant. For consciousness, you can stay agnostic: nothing forces you to claim that a system which behaves intelligently also feels anything. So the right posture is "functionalism for intelligence, open verdict on consciousness." That is not a dodge. It is the most defensible position available, and it lets you win the argument that matters for this course without overclaiming on the one that is genuinely unsettled.

What functionalism actually says

The idea has a specific origin. Hilary Putnam laid it out in a 1967 paper originally titled "Psychological Predicates" and later reprinted as "The Nature of Mental States" (Putnam, 1967). Putnam was attacking a view that was popular at the time: the idea that a mental state simply is a particular physical state of the brain — that, say, pain is identical to a specific firing pattern of C-fibers in human nervous tissue. His objection was devastating in its simplicity. Octopuses feel pain. So, plausibly, do birds, whose brains are wired completely differently from mammals'. If pain were literally identical to one specific neural arrangement, then any creature lacking that exact arrangement could not be in pain — which is absurd. The same mental state shows up in radically different physical hardware.

Putnam's fix was to define mental states one level up. A mental state is not a lump of matter; it is a functional role. Pain is whatever state is typically caused by bodily damage, tends to cause wincing and avoidance, raises the priority of escaping the source, and combines with the belief "this is harmful" to produce the desire to make it stop. Anything — carbon, silicon, octopus — that occupies that role is in pain, by definition. Philosophers call this property multiple realizability: the same function can be realized in many different physical media. A mousetrap can be wood-and-spring or a glue board or a bucket of water; what makes each one a mousetrap is the role it plays, not the materials. Putnam's move was to say minds are like that too.

Here it helps to name the rival directly, because the first verification question turns on the contrast. The view functionalism opposes most sharply is biological naturalism, associated with the philosopher John Searle. Biological naturalism holds that consciousness and genuine understanding are biological phenomena, produced by the specific causal powers of living brain tissue, the way digestion is produced by the specific causal powers of a stomach. On this view substrate is not incidental — it is the whole point. You can simulate a stomach in software, Searle would say, but the simulation does not digest a real sandwich; it just computes what digestion would do. By analogy, you can simulate a mind in silicon, but the simulation does not understand anything; it only goes through the motions. So the two camps disagree at the root:

  • Functionalism: a mental state is its functional role. Get the role right in any material and you get the genuine article. Substrate is irrelevant.
  • Biological naturalism: mental states arise from the specific causal powers of biological neurons. Get the role right in the wrong material and you get a clever imitation, not the real thing. Substrate is essential.

That single disagreement — is the material incidental or essential? — is the hinge the rest of this page swings on.

Why this is the deepest objection LLMs face

Every other skeptical argument in this course has been about capability. "It can't really reason." "It just memorized." "It has no world model." Those are empirical claims, and the earlier modules answered them with evidence — you saw in G34 — Internal World Models that models build genuine internal representations of the situations they describe, and in G5 — Emergent Capabilities that new abilities appear at scale. The substrate objection is different in kind. It concedes the capability and attacks anyway. It says: "Fine. Grant that it reasons, plans, models the world, passes every test. It is still just silicon, just math, just matrix multiplications — so whatever it's doing, it isn't really thinking." This is the wall a skeptic retreats to when all the empirical walls have fallen. It is the last objection, and it is the one functionalism was built to answer.

This is exactly why substrate independence matters so much for evaluating LLM intelligence — the second verification question. If intelligence is substrate-dependent — if it requires carbon, requires neurons, requires the wet electrochemistry of a living brain — then no amount of performance could ever count as real intelligence in a machine, because the machine is made of the wrong stuff. The verdict would be settled before you ran a single test. The argument would be unfalsifiable: every success the model posts gets waved away with "yes, but it's silicon." Substrate independence is what keeps the question honest. It says: judge intelligence by what a system does, not by what it's made of, the same way you judge a calculator by its answers and not its casing. Without that principle, "is it intelligent?" stops being a question about the system and becomes a question about your prejudice over its materials.

Notice what substrate independence does not commit you to. It does not say the model is conscious, or that it has feelings, or that there is something it is like to be the model. It only says that being made of silicon is not, by itself, a disqualifier. That is a modest claim, and it is the one doing all the work.

The calculator test: same function, different substrate

Let's make the anchor analogy carry real weight, because it is the cleanest demonstration of why function can be separated from material.

Physical Calculator

A dedicated electronic device. Inside: a hard-wired arithmetic logic unit etched into a custom chip, a seven-segment LED display, a coin battery or solar strip. Built in a factory for exactly one purpose. Press 347, ×, 28, =. The circuit shuttles voltages through transistors arranged to implement binary multiplication. Output: 9,716.

Calculator App

Software running on a general-purpose phone. No arithmetic circuit dedicated to the job — instead, lines of code interpreted by a processor that could just as easily be running a game or a browser. Tap 347, ×, 28, =. The CPU executes instructions that implement binary multiplication. Output: 9,716.

Ask the question that matters: did both of them multiply? Not "did both produce the same number by coincidence" — we know they didn't, because you could feed them any of a billion inputs and they'd agree every time. They agree because both implement the same function: the mapping from a pair of numbers to their product. The physical materials are completely different. One is a single-purpose circuit; the other is software riding on hardware designed for nothing in particular. Yet multiplication — the actual capacity, not a fake of it — is present in both. The function is what makes it multiplication. The substrate is a delivery vehicle.

Push it harder. Multiplication also happens when a person works it out with pencil and paper, carrying digits across columns. It happens on a Babbage-style brass gear machine. It happens with an abacus, beads sliding on rods. Wood, brass, neurons, silicon, software — five substrates, one function, and in every case we are entirely comfortable saying real multiplication occurred. Nobody insists that only the brass machine "really" multiplies because it has the most satisfying mechanism. Functionalism simply asks you to extend that comfort upward: if multiplication is multiply-realizable, why not reasoning? Why not understanding? Why not, eventually, mind? The burden flips onto the skeptic to explain what is so special about these particular functions that they alone refuse to travel across substrates.

Functionalism's home-field advantage — and where the ground gets soft

It's worth being clear about how mainstream this position is. Functionalism is, by most counts, the dominant view in contemporary philosophy of mind — the default framework most working philosophers and cognitive scientists operate within, even when they quarrel about the details. That status isn't an accident. Functionalism is the natural philosophy of cognitive science itself, which routinely explains mental processes as information-processing roles ("working memory," "attention," "the visual buffer") without first asking what the tissue is made of. When a neuroscientist models a decision as the accumulation of evidence toward a threshold, they are doing functionalism. The view earned its dominance by being the one that lets the science proceed.

But "dominant" is not "settled," and this course doesn't oversell. Functionalism has a soft spot, and it is precisely located. The view works beautifully for capacities defined entirely by their input-output behavior — arithmetic, logical inference, planning, language production. For those, "getting the function right" plausibly leaves nothing out. The trouble starts when you turn from what the system does to whether there's anything it's like to be the system. And that is where the strongest objection lives.

The strongest objection: qualia and the absent feeling

The third verification question asks for the strongest argument against functionalism, stated fairly — and intellectual honesty requires giving it real teeth before answering. The objection centers on qualia: the raw, subjective, felt qualities of experience. The redness of red. The throb of a stubbed toe. The particular sourness of biting a lemon. These are the "what-it's-like" aspects of mental states, and the worry is that functionalism cannot capture them, because they seem to be left over after you've specified every functional role.

The objection is usually dramatized with thought experiments, and they are genuinely unsettling. Consider the inverted spectrum: imagine a person whose color experience is flipped, so that when they look at a ripe tomato they have the inner experience you'd call "green," yet they learned the word "red" for it, call grass "green" too, stop at red lights, and behave identically to you in every measurable way. Functionally, you are indistinguishable. Same inputs, same outputs, same causal roles. But by hypothesis your inner experiences are different. If that scenario is even coherent, then function does not fix experience — something about the felt quality slips through the functional net.

A second version cuts deeper: the philosophical zombie. Imagine a being functionally identical to a human in every respect — it talks about its feelings, flinches from pain, debates philosophy — but with the lights off inside. No inner experience at all. Pure mechanism, zero felt life. If such a zombie is even conceivable, then all the functional facts can be in place while the experiential facts are absent — which would mean experience is not just a matter of function. This is the engine behind David Chalmers' "hard problem of consciousness" that you met in G10: explaining behavior is the easy problem; explaining why any of it is felt is the hard one, and functionalism, the objection says, only ever addresses the easy one.

Take this seriously. The qualia objection is not a confused person's mistake; it is the single most discussed problem in philosophy of mind, defended by serious thinkers, and it has no knockdown refutation. If you walk away from this page thinking functionalism has been proven, you've misread it. The honest summary is that functionalism is the leading account and the qualia objection is the leading reason some smart people reject it. The debate is live.

How a functionalist responds — without pretending to win

A good functionalist doesn't fold here; they push back along three lines, and you should know them because together they show why the objection wounds without killing.

  1. Deny the scenarios are coherent. The functionalist can argue that a true zombie or a stable inverted spectrum is only apparently conceivable. We can say the words "a being just like me but with no experience," but maybe we can't actually conceive it any more than we can conceive a four-sided triangle — the felt quality might be constituted by the functional organization, so that fixing the function fixes the feeling whether our imagination cooperates or not. Conceivability, on this line, is a fact about our imagination, not about what's possible.
  2. Quarantine the damage. Even granting that qualia escape the functional net, notice what's left untouched. The objection targets consciousness — the felt quality — not intelligence, the problem-solving capacity. And thanks to the G10 split, those come apart. So a functionalist can cheerfully concede the qualia point and still hold the line that matters for this course: silicon intelligence is real intelligence, because intelligence is functional even if experience might not be. The objection, at its most successful, costs you consciousness and leaves intelligence standing.
  3. Ask what the alternative explains. Biological naturalism says the magic is in the carbon — but it never says which property of carbon does the trick, or why simulated neurons that reproduce every causal interaction would fail to reproduce the result. "It has to be biological" can start to look less like an explanation and more like a restatement of the intuition it was supposed to justify.

None of these three is a finishing blow, and a careful functionalist admits it. The point isn't to declare victory over qualia. It's to show that the objection, even at full strength, doesn't reach the claim you actually need: that a system's intelligence should be judged by what it does, not what it's made of.

Bringing it home to the machine

So line up the whole argument. The skeptic's last redoubt is "it's just silicon." Functionalism answers: substrate is, for the things we care about most, irrelevant — what defines a mental state is the role it plays, and roles are multiply realizable, as the humblest calculator demonstrates. That position is the mainstream view in philosophy of mind, not a fringe convenience invented to flatter machines. Its hardest critics attack it through qualia — the felt quality of experience — and that attack is real, unresolved, and worth respecting. But the attack lands on consciousness, which the G10 split lets you set aside, and it leaves intelligence — the capacity this entire course is about — right where it stood.

That is the disciplined conclusion. Not "the model definitely has a rich inner life." Rather: "the fact that it's made of silicon is not a reason to deny its intelligence, and the burden is on the skeptic to say why these functions, alone among all functions we know, refuse to cross substrates." When someone reaches for "it's just silicon," you now have the reply — and you have it without overclaiming a single thing about what the machine feels.

One thread stays loose on purpose, and it leads to the next page. Even if we grant that the model's states play genuine functional roles, there's a further question lurking: when the model produces the sentence "Paris is the capital of France," are its internal states genuinely about Paris the way your thought is about Paris — or are they just symbols pushed around with no real reference to the world? That question of aboutness is the subject of G31 — Intentionality and Aboutness, and it's where Searle returns with his most famous weapon, the Chinese Room.

Key Insight

Functionalism reframes "can silicon think?" the way a calculator reframes "can plastic multiply?" — you judge a mental state by the functional role it plays (inputs, outputs, causal relations), not by the material that implements it. Because roles are multiply realizable, substrate is irrelevant to intelligence. The strongest objection — qualia, the felt quality of experience — is real and unresolved, but it targets consciousness, not intelligence. So the defensible verdict is: being made of silicon is no reason to deny a system's intelligence, even if it's no proof the system feels anything.
